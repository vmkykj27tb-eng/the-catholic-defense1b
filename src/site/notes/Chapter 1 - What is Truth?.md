When Pontius Pilate sneered in Christs face and asked, "What is truth?" (John 18:38), he was using the same skepticism that proves strong in the world we have today. In a culture driven by moral relativism-the false idea that "what is truth for you isn't truth for me." The Catholic Church relies on the correspondence theory of truth that was written by Saint Thomas Aquinas that states something is true if it is actually true in simple terms. Truth cannot be determined by feelings, how many people are believers of it, or trends. If something reflects truthfully the way things are, it is just objectively true for everyone, all places, at all times. 


Scripture and Catholic theology reject the idea that truth can be flexible. Instead, the Catholic Church teaches that truth is anchored in the nature of God. As the Catechism of the Catholic Church states in CCC 215, "God is Truth itself, whose words cannot deceive. This is why one can abandon oneself in full trust to the truth and faithfulness of his word in all things."


Because God is the true creator of all things, everything that exists is really just a reflection of what he thinks, human reason and mind power is capable of discovering truth by truly seeking God. In original sin, however, our intellect was damaged due to the disobedience from Eve. Our reasoning is naturally confused and faulty, which is why we need to rely on God and divine revelation to know what's indeed true. God broke into human history through his son Jesus Christ and inspiration through all of the prophets and saints to reveal truths that we as humans cannot naturally see. 


Ultimately, our apologetics as Catholics takes more reasoning to understand. Whereas other apologetics or belief systems are just cold things you need to believe to follow the religion that aren't fact but are theory. In John 14:6 Jesus makes a claim to absolute divinity and him being the truth, "I am the way, the truth, and the life; no one comes to the father, but by me."
