The skeptic always always loves to throw in the problem of evil in debates if it is a checkmate against Christians. They assert that if God is all good, then He woudl want to get rid of evil completely, and if He is all powerful, he could get rid of evil, so they conclude because evil exists, therefore an all powerful and good God doesn't exist. This is a blunder, however, as they fail to see that by using the exact word "evil" they destroy the arguments of Atheism. Frank Sheed noted how you can't define something as bad unless you have a concept of what is perfectly good. If the universe is godless and just a project of matter, than suffering and cruelty aren't inherently evil. They just happen to be natural physics (like they are within animals). By crying about "bu bu why does God let muh evil happen," they destroy their own argument by demanding an objective goodness which they acknowledge, which can only happen with God. 


To get out of such a trap in a debate, you have to use a definition of what evil is. Saint Augustine helped  engineer the debate by showing how evil isn't a thing created by God. God created everything, and everything he created is good. Evil is just a corruption of good that should be there. Blindness isn't an object, it is just an absence of sight. Using the same logic, evil is just the absence of love and moral good in a humans free will. God didn't create evil, us and the fallen angels created evil by misusing their free will to turn from God. 


In the Catechism of the Catholic Church in CCC 309, the answer to the problem of evil is the entire Christian message. Critics demand to know why God doesn't just get rid of all evil in a snap of the finger. If God got rid of all evil immediately, he would have to get rid of EVERY HUMAN BEING who was committed sin or thought sin. That would be every one of us currently on the planet which would be terrible. God delays the final judgment because he wants to give us time to repent to let as many people to be saved as possible. He gave us free will so we could choose him, and he could see his true followers and those who choose satan. 

The Catholic response to suffering is found in the Crucifixion. Saint Thomas Aquinas said that God is so powerful he allows evil because he can bring a greater good out of it. God isn't distant or uncaring, he is infact the opposite. God literally came down and died the worst possible death for our salvation. Jesus took his death for the salvation of humanity and redeemed the race. The problem of evil doesn't do anything to God, it just helps the case for God and Catholicism.
